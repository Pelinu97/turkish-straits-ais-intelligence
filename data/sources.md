# Open-Source Data & References

All sources used in this project are publicly available. No classified or proprietary information has been used, and this project does not operate independent AIS receivers or hold paid institutional data feeds — all AIS-derived claims trace back to the free-tier platforms or named research organizations listed below.

---

## AIS & Maritime Tracking Platforms

- [MarineTraffic](https://www.marinetraffic.com) — real-time and historical AIS vessel tracking (free tier used; historical depth limited compared to paid access)
- [VesselFinder](https://www.vesselfinder.com) — AIS vessel tracking and port data
- [MyShipTracking](https://www.myshiptracking.com) — real-time maritime tracking
- [Bosphorus Observer](https://twitter.com/BosphorusObserv) — volunteer-run, open-source strait monitoring; treated as a single, uncorroborated source unless cross-checked against another platform or report

---

## Government & Official Sources

- Montreux Convention (1936) — full text, available via multiple treaty-archive sources
- Turkish Ministry of Foreign Affairs — statements on Straits policy ([mfa.gov.tr](https://www.mfa.gov.tr))
- IMO — AIS technical standards ([imo.org](https://www.imo.org))
- UN Black Sea Grain Initiative — official documentation ([un.org](https://www.un.org))
- U.S. Department of the Treasury — OFAC sanctions list ([ofac.treasury.gov](https://ofac.treasury.gov))

## Methodological Note

Where a finding in `/analysis` depends on a single source (most notably Bosphorus Observer, a volunteer-run account), this is flagged explicitly in the relevant document rather than presented with the same confidence as multi-source-corroborated findings. The Berdyansk/Orsk case (Analysis 3, Case Study 1) is a specific reminder built into this project: early viral reporting named the wrong vessel as destroyed, and that correction is treated as authoritative here even though the original version remains more widely circulated.
