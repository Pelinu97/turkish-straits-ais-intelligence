# Turkish Straits Overview: Strategic and Geopolitical Significance

## Overview

The Turkish Straits — comprising the Bosphorus and the Dardanelles — are among the most strategically significant waterways in the world, connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean and serving as a corridor for military, commercial, and energy-related maritime traffic.

## Geographic Significance

- **Bosphorus**: approximately 31 km long, with a minimum width of roughly 700 meters, running through Istanbul
- **Dardanelles**: approximately 61 km long, connecting the Aegean Sea to the Sea of Marmara
- The straits are consistently cited as among the world's busiest and most navigationally demanding waterways, given dense traffic combined with sharp turns and strong currents
- Annual vessel-transit figures vary by year and source; figures in the tens of thousands are commonly cited by Turkish maritime authorities and should be sourced to a specific year when used (see Limitations)

## Legal Framework: Montreux Convention (1936)

The Montreux Convention governs passage through the straits:

- Commercial vessels generally have freedom of passage in peacetime
- Warships of non-Black Sea states face tonnage and transit-duration limits
- Turkey has the right to restrict passage during wartime or when it considers itself threatened with imminent danger of war
- In February 2022, following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Turkey formally notified Black Sea states that it was applying Convention provisions restricting warship passage — commonly described in reporting as a "closure" to belligerent warships, with some nuance in how strictly that term should be applied (see Case Study 1 in Analysis 3)

## Strategic Importance

### Energy Transit
- A significant corridor for Russian, Kazakh, and Azerbaijani oil exports
- Specific daily-barrel figures fluctuate with market and geopolitical conditions; any figure cited should be dated to its source rather than treated as a constant

### Military Significance
- The straits are Russia's Black Sea Fleet's only route to the Mediterranean
- Widely discussed as part of NATO's southeastern maritime flank
- Turkey's control over passage gives it meaningful geopolitical leverage, exercised most visibly through its 2022 Montreux notifications

### Commercial Traffic
- A key route for Ukrainian and Russian grain exports, particularly relevant during the 2022–2023 Black Sea Grain Initiative (see Analysis 3, Case Study 3)
- Substantial container and bulk-carrier traffic between Europe and Asia

## OSINT Relevance

Open-source AIS data, as used by the platforms and researchers cited throughout this project, allows analysts to:

- Track vessel movements with a time lag (free-tier platforms are not always real-time)
- Identify deviations from typical traffic patterns, as documented in published case studies
- Cross-reference reported transits against Montreux Convention notifications
- Identify candidate vessels for further investigation into sanctions evasion or dual-use cargo — typically requiring cross-referencing with registries and reporting beyond AIS data alone

## Limitations

- Vessel-transit and oil-flow figures change over time; this document avoids treating any single statistic as current without a source year, and figures should be re-verified against a dated source before use in any application context
- "Closure" of the straits under Montreux is a matter of some legal and practical nuance; this document follows common reporting usage while noting the underlying legal mechanism is a transit restriction tied to wartime provisions, not an absolute seal
