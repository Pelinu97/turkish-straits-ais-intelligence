# Vessel Tracking Case Studies: Turkish Straits

## Overview

This section presents three case studies of documented vessel-movement events connected to the Turkish Straits, based on publicly available reporting and AIS-platform data. Confidence levels reflect source corroboration, not original investigation by this project.

---

## Case Study 1: Montreux Convention Closure and Black Sea Fleet Access (2022)

**Confidence: Medium.** The Montreux notification and its broad effect are well-documented; specific vessel-level claims require more caution, as illustrated below.

### Background
Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Turkey formally notified Black Sea states that it was applying Montreux Convention provisions restricting warship passage through the straits.

### Key Findings
- Turkey notified Russia, Ukraine, and other Black Sea states of the restriction on February 28, 2022
- Some Russian naval vessels were reported transiting the Bosphorus in the days immediately before the notification took effect, per AIS-platform and observer reporting
- A widely reported strike on Russian landing ships at the occupied port of Berdyansk occurred in late March 2022. **Initial reporting (including from Ukrainian officials) named the destroyed vessel as the *Orsk*; Ukrainian armed forces subsequently corrected this, identifying the destroyed ship as the *Saratov*, with the *Orsk* and a third landing ship (*Novocherkassk* / *Caesar Kunikov*, per varying reports) damaged rather than destroyed.** This correction is documented by multiple outlets (CNN, Wikipedia's sourced summary of the incident) and is noted here specifically because the *Orsk*-destroyed version circulated widely and is still sometimes repeated uncorrected.
- This event was documented via social media video, subsequently verified by outlets including Reuters and BBC — not via AIS or satellite tracking, despite that method sometimes being assumed in casual summaries of the incident
- With the strait route restricted to Russian warships, Russia's Black Sea Fleet lost the ability to receive naval reinforcements via the Bosphorus for the duration of the restriction

### Sources
- Reuters, CNN, BBC — Berdyansk port attack reporting (March 2022)
- Wikipedia — "Berdiansk port attack" (useful as a sourced summary; underlying citations should be checked directly for any claim used further)
- Bosphorus Observer — Twitter/X feed (single-source; treat vessel-transit claims as indicative pending corroboration)
- MarineTraffic — historical AIS data, where accessible
- Turkish Ministry of Foreign Affairs — official statements on Montreux application

## Case Study 2: "Shadow Fleet" Oil Tankers (2023–2024)

**Confidence: Medium-High**, grounded in specific, named-organization reporting (CREA, Lloyd's List Intelligence, Financial Times); lower confidence for any aggregate claims not directly sourced to one of these.

### Background
Following Western sanctions on Russian oil exports, researchers and journalists documented a growing fleet of aging, often obscurely flagged tankers transporting Russian crude, with the Turkish Straits as a key transit route.

### Key Findings
- Multiple research organizations (CREA, Lloyd's List Intelligence) documented increased tanker traffic from Russian Black Sea ports via AIS-based tracking
- Reporting identified vessels flagged under registries including Gabon, Palau, and Cameroon — commonly described as flags of convenience in this context
- Specific vessels were identified in reporting as having disabled or manipulated AIS signals at points in their voyages
- Insurance-related reporting (Financial Times, Lloyd's List) noted some vessels operating without standard Western P&I (Protection & Indemnity) club coverage, raising environmental-liability concerns flagged by these outlets
- Turkey's position, per its own statements, has been that Montreux Convention obligations require it to permit transit it cannot unilaterally restrict on sanctions grounds alone; this is Turkey's stated position as reported, not an independent legal assessment by this project

### Sources
- CREA (Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air) — shadow-fleet tracking reports
- Lloyd's List Intelligence — sanctions and insurance-status tracking
- Financial Times — shadow-fleet investigative reporting
- MarineTraffic — vessel history and flag-state data

## Case Study 3: Black Sea Grain Initiative Monitoring (2022–2023)

**Confidence: Medium-High** for the existence and basic mechanics of the Initiative (UN-documented); **Medium** for specific vessel-count figures, which should be sourced to a specific reporting date rather than treated as fixed.

### Background
The Black Sea Grain Initiative, brokered by the UN and Turkey in July 2022, established a mechanism for Ukrainian grain exports to resume through the Turkish Straits via a Joint Coordination Centre (JCC) based in Istanbul.

### Key Findings
- The JCC, involving Russia, Ukraine, Turkey, and the UN, monitored vessel movements under the Initiative
- UN reporting documents a substantial number of vessel transits under the Initiative over its roughly one-year operation; the specific cumulative figure should be cited to a dated UN/JCC report rather than repeated as a static number
- AIS-derived tracking, as reported by monitoring platforms, was used informally to observe compliance with designated corridor routes
- Russia's withdrawal from the Initiative in July 2023 was followed by a documented decline in grain-corridor vessel traffic, as reported by Reuters and other outlets tracking the corridor

### Sources
- UN Joint Coordination Centre — official documentation and statements
- Reuters — Black Sea Grain Initiative reporting
- MarineTraffic — grain-corridor-related vessel data, where accessible
- Bosphorus Observer — supplementary transit monitoring (single-source; see caveat in Case Study 1)

## Summary

These case studies illustrate the value of open-source AIS data and platform-based reporting for understanding maritime developments connected to the Turkish Straits, while also illustrating a key risk: early, unverified claims (such as the initial "Orsk destroyed" reporting) can circulate well past the point of correction. This project treats corrected/updated reporting as authoritative and flags cases where an earlier, less accurate version of events is still commonly repeated.
