# Vessel Tracking Case Studies: Turkish Straits

## Overview

This section presents open-source case studies of notable vessel movements through the Turkish Straits, based entirely on publicly available AIS data and open-source reporting.

---

## Case Study 1: Russian Black Sea Fleet Movements (2022)

### Background
Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Turkey invoked the Montreux Convention and closed the straits to warships of all belligerent nations.

### Key Findings
- Turkey formally notified Russia, Ukraine, and other Black Sea states of strait closure on February 28, 2022
- Several Russian naval vessels were tracked transiting the Bosphorus in the days immediately before the closure
- The Russian landing ship *Orsk* was subsequently destroyed in Berdyansk port — tracked via open-source satellite imagery
- Russia's Black Sea Fleet was effectively trapped, unable to receive naval reinforcements through the straits

### OSINT Sources Used
- Bosphorus Observer Twitter/X feed
- MarineTraffic historical AIS data
- Bellingcat — open-source naval tracking
- Turkish Ministry of Foreign Affairs official statements

## Case Study 2: Shadow Fleet Oil Tankers (2023–2024)

### Background
Following Western sanctions on Russian oil, a "shadow fleet" of aging, obscurely flagged tankers emerged to transport Russian crude through the Turkish Straits.

### Key Findings
- Significant increase in tanker traffic from Russian Black Sea ports detected via AIS
- Many vessels flagged under Gabon, Palau, and Cameroon — flags of convenience
- Several tankers identified as having disabled or spoofed AIS signals
- Insurance records showed vessels operating without Western P&I club coverage
- Turkey faced Western pressure to restrict passage but cited Montreux Convention obligations

### OSINT Sources Used
- MarineTraffic — vessel history and flag tracking
- Lloyd's List Intelligence — sanctions tracking
- CREA — Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air
- Financial Times — shadow fleet investigations

## Case Study 3: Grain Corridor Monitoring (2022–2023)

### Background
The Black Sea Grain Initiative, brokered by the UN and Turkey in July 2022, allowed Ukrainian grain exports to resume through the Turkish Straits.

### Key Findings
- Joint Coordination Centre established in Istanbul to monitor vessel movements
- Over 1,000 vessels tracked transiting under the initiative
- AIS data used to verify compliance with designated safe corridors
- Russia's withdrawal from the initiative in July 2023 immediately visible through AIS traffic decline

### OSINT Sources Used
- UN Joint Coordination Centre — public vessel tracking
- MarineTraffic — grain corridor vessel data
- Bosphorus Observer — real-time transit monitoring
- Reuters — grain initiative reporting

## Summary

These case studies demonstrate the intelligence value of open-source AIS data in monitoring strategically significant maritime movements. The Turkish Straits serve as a critical observation point for Black Sea geopolitics, energy flows, and sanctions enforcement.
